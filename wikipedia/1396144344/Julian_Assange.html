Julian Paul Assange (born 3 July 1971, Townsville, Queensland) is an Australian publisher and journalist best known as the editor-in-chief of the whistleblower website WikiLeaks, which he co-founded in 2006 after an earlier career in hacking and programming. WikiLeaks achieved particular prominence in 2010 when it published U.S. military and diplomatic documents leaked by Chelsea Manning. Assange has been under investigation in the United States since that time. In the same year he was accused of rape and other sexual offences in Sweden. In 2012, facing extradition to Sweden, he took refuge at the Ecuadorian embassy in London. There he remains, having been granted political asylum by Ecuador.

Early life

Assange was born in Townsville.

Assange was born in the north Queensland city of Townsville[1][2] to Christine Ann Assange (née Hawkins) (b. 1951),[3] an aspiring visual artist[4][5] who "had modelled and acted,"[6] and John Shipton, an anti-war activist, builder, and onetime architecture student.[7] The couple ended their relationship early in the pregnancy.[7] Assange's maternal grandfather was educator Dr Warren Alfred Hawkins (1924–2012).[8] When he was a year old, his mother married Richard Brett Assange,[9][10][11] with whom she ran "a small eccentric theatre company,"[12] "presenting children's theatre and political agitprop,"[13] and "later involving puppets."[14] In 1979, she married cult member Leif Meynall, also known as Leif Hamilton,[15][13] with whom she had a son before the couple broke up in 1982.[1] Owing partly to his mother's wanderlust, partly to her work, and partly to her later fear of Meynall-Hamilton,[16][17] Assange had a nomadic childhood, claiming to have lived in thirty[18] or fifty[19] different Australian towns, from Townsville in the north-east to Perth in the south-west,[20][21] by the time he reached his mid-teens, when he settled with his mother and step-brother in Melbourne, Victoria.[22][23] He attended many schools, including Goolmangar Primary School in New South Wales (1979–1983)[12] and Townsville State High School,[24] as well as being schooled at home.[9] He studied programming, mathematics, and physics at Central Queensland University (1994),[25] the University of Melbourne (2003–2006),[26][27][13][22][18] and possibly other universities (he claims to have attended six),[28] but did not complete a degree.[29]

Hacking

In 1987, Assange began hacking under the name Mendax (from Horace's "splendide mendax": "nobly untruthful").[9][30] He and two others—known as Trax and Prime Suspect—formed an ethical hacking group they called the International Subversives.[9] During this time he hacked into the Pentagon and other U.S. Department of Defense facilities, MILNET, the U.S. Navy, NASA, and Australia's Overseas Telecommunications Commission; Lockheed Martin, Motorola, Panasonic, Xerox, and Citibank; and Stanford University's Stanford Research Institute, the Australian National University, and La Trobe University.[31][32][9][33] He is thought to have been involved in the WANK (Worms Against Nuclear Killers) hack at NASA in 1989, but he does not acknowledge this.[34][35]

In September 1991, he was discovered hacking into the Melbourne master terminal of Nortel, a Canadian telecommunications company.[9] The Australian Federal Police tapped Assange's phone line (he was using a modem), raided his Melbourne home at the end of October,[36][37] and eventually charged him in 1994 with thirty-one counts of hacking and related crimes.[9] Trax and Prime Suspect were each charged with a smaller number of offences.[38] In May 1996, he pleaded guilty to twenty-five charges (the other six were dropped), and was fined A$2,100 and released on a good behaviour bond,[39][9][40][34] avoiding a heavier penalty thanks to the absence of malicious or mercenary intent and his disrupted childhood.[41][42]

Programming

In 1993, Assange gave technical advice to the Victoria Police Child Exploitation Unit and assisted with prosecutions.[43] In the same year he was involved in starting one of the first public internet service providers in Australia, Suburbia Public Access Network.[22][44] He began programming in 1994, authoring or co-authoring the port scanner Strobe (1995),[45][46] patches to the open-source database PostgreSQL (1996),[47][48] the Usenet caching software NNTPCache (1996),[49] the Rubberhose deniable encryption system (1997),[50][51] which reflected his growing interest in cryptography,[52] and Surfraw, a command-line interface for web-based search engines (2000).[53] During this period he also moderated the AUCRYPTO forum,[52] ran Best of Security, a website "giving advice on computer security," which had 5,000 subscribers in 1996,[54] and contributed research to Suelette Dreyfus's Underground (1997), a book about Australian hackers, including the International Subversives.[30][55] In 1998, he co-founded the company Earthmen Technology.[41]

In 1999, he registered the domain leaks.org, but, as he put it, "I didn't do anything with it."[41] He did, however, publicise a patent granted to the National Security Agency in August 1999 for voice-data harvesting technology: "This patent should worry people. Everyone's overseas phone calls are or may soon be tapped, transcribed and archived in the bowels of an unaccountable foreign spy agency."[52] This would remain an abiding concern, to which he returned more than a decade later in Cypherpunks (2012), foreseeing a dystopian future in which, "the Internet, our greatest tool for emancipation, has been transformed into the most dangerous facilitator of totalitarianism we have ever seen,"[56] and anticipating Edward Snowden's revelations the following year. Little of his work during this period was paid, and for much of the nineties Assange relied on government single-parent benefits.[57][40]

WikiLeaks

Assange, c. 2006
Main article: WikiLeaks

After his period of study at the University of Melbourne, Assange and others established WikiLeaks in 2006. Assange is a member of the organisation's advisory board[58] and styles himself its editor-in-chief.[59] From this time on he was continuously on the move, with periods in Kenya,[19][60] Tanzania,[60] Egypt,[61] France,[60] and Germany[61][62] (2007–2008); Austria,[63] Spain,[64] Malaysia,[65] and Denmark[66] (2009); and Iceland,[9][61][67] the United States,[68][69][70] Norway,[71] Australia,[70] Belgium,[72][73] Sweden,[74] and the United Kingdom[75][76] (2010). The purpose was to develop the organisation’s infrastructure, promote its activities at conferences and through the media, and further his editorial work.

WikiLeaks posted large amounts of material exposing government and corporate wrongdoing between 2006 and 2009, attracting various degrees of publicity.[77] Nevertheless, the organisation "was still struggling" and Assange was "hunting vainly for a WikiLeaks model that could both bring in working revenue and gain global political attention."[78] It was only when it began publishing documents supplied by Chelsea Manning that it became a household name.[79] The Manning material included the Collateral Murder video (April 2010), an edited version of which was viewed 14.5 million times on YouTube over the next four years,[80] the Guantánamo files (April 2010), the Afghanistan war logs (July 2010), the Iraq war logs (October 2010), and a quarter of a million diplomatic cables (November 2010). WikiLeaks was criticised by Amnesty International and other human rights groups for failing to remove identifying information from the Afghanistan war logs.[81] It took greater care with the Iraq war logs,[82] and seemed set to do the same with the diplomatic cables until Assange released them unredacted after getting the go-ahead from his Twitter followers.[83][84]

Opinions of Assange at this time were divided. Australian Prime Minister Julia Gillard described his activities as "illegal",[85] only to be told that he had broken no Australian law.[86] U.S. Vice President Joe Biden and others called him a "terrorist".[87][88][89][90][91] Some demanded his assassination or execution.[92][93][94][95] Support came from Brazilian President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva,[96][97] Russian Prime Minister Vladimir Putin,[98][99] and activists and celebrities including Tariq Ali,[100] the Electronic Frontier Foundation's John Perry Barlow,[101] Pentagon Papers leaker Daniel Ellsberg,[102][103] the Swedish Pirate Party's Rick Falkvinge,[104][105] Bianca Jagger,[106] Jemima Khan[107] (who has since had a change of heart),[108] Mary Kostakidis,[109] Ken Loach,[107] Michael Moore,[110][111] John Pilger,[107][112][113] the Frontline Club's Vaughan Smith,[114][115] Oliver Stone,[111] and Naomi Wolf.[116][117][118]

The annus mirabilis of 2010 culminated with the Sam Adams Award, which Assange accepted in October,[119] and a string of distinctions in December—the Le Monde readers' choice award for person of the year,[120][121] the Time readers' choice award for person of the year (he was also a runner-up in Time's overall person of the year award),[122][123] a US$1.3 million deal for his autobiography,[124][125][126] and selection by the Italian edition of Rolling Stone as "rockstar of the year."[127][128]

The following February he won the Sydney Peace Foundation Gold Medal for Peace with Justice, previously awarded to only three people—Nelson Mandela, the Dalai Lama, and Buddhist spiritual leader Daisaku Ikeda.[129] Two weeks later he filed for the trademark "Julian Assange" in Europe, which was to be used for "Public speaking services; news reporter services; journalism; publication of texts other than publicity texts; education services; entertainment services."[130][131][132] For several years a member of the Australian journalists' union and still an honorary member,[133][134][135] he picked up the Martha Gellhorn Prize for Journalism in June,[136][137] and the Walkley Award for Most Outstanding Contribution to Journalism in November,[138][139] having earlier won the Amnesty International UK Media Award (New Media) in 2009.[140] Three books about him were published during 2011: WikiLeaks: Inside Julian Assange's War on Secrecy by David Leigh and Luke Harding (February);[141] The Most Dangerous Man in the World by Andrew Fowler (April);[142] and Julian Assange: The Unauthorised Autobiography by Andrew O'Hagan (September).[143]

U.S. legal position

There were two avenues open to U.S. authorities under the Espionage Act of 1917 after WikiLeaks released the Manning material—they might try to prosecute Assange for publishing it, or they might try to prosecute him for inciting, abetting, or conspiring with Manning to obtain it.[144]

The difficulty with the first approach was that the Manning material had been carried by many media outlets, including the New York Times, the Washington Post, and the Guardian, and it would be untenable to pursue one without pursuing all.[145] Journalists and publishers in the U.S. are also protected by the First Amendment guarantee of free speech, which makes it unlikely that such a prosecution would succeed.[146][147] WikiLeaks' First Amendment immunity was recognised as early as 2008 by the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California in Bank Julius Baer v. WikiLeaks.[148][149] No one has been put on trial simply for publishing the leaks.

The difficulty with the second approach was that the offence had to be proven. If it were, Assange could expect punishment proportional to Manning's thirty-five-year jail term.[150] The same precedent seemed to rule out the death penalty or transportation to Guantánamo,[151][152] concerns that had already been dismissed as "ridiculous".[151][153] In any event, there was no certainty that the U.S. government would get its way, as the example of Thomas Drake showed.[154][155][156]

In November 2010, U.S. Attorney-General Eric Holder said there was "an active, ongoing criminal investigation" into WikiLeaks.[157] It emerged from legal documents leaked over the ensuing months that Assange and others were being investigated by a federal grand jury in Alexandria, Virginia, although it is unclear when it was impanelled (a vague reference in a leaked email from September 2010 to "grand jury matters" is inconclusive).[158][159][160] Like all grand juries, it was operating in camera, so there is no public record of its deliberations.

A one-line email from an employee of intelligence consultancy Strategic Forecasting, Inc. (Stratfor) leaked in 2012 said, "We have a sealed indictment on Assange."[161] This has been dismissed as evidence of "how a Texas-based corporate research firm can get a little carried away in marketing itself,"[162] and, like all the leaked Stratfor material, as inconsequential and oversold "gossip."[162][163][164][165] There were other grounds for scepticism. First, the email was sent in January 2011, when the grand jury was still in its infancy, and before what appears to have been its main period of activity later that year.[166] Second, in December 2011, prosecution filings at a pre-trial hearing for Chelsea Manning indicated that there were no U.S. charges against Assange at that time.[166] Third, the grand jury was still impanelled in November 2013, nearly three years after the Stratfor employee boasted that charges had been finalised.[145] U.S. authorities have consistently denied the existence of such an indictment.[167]

Meanwhile, the argument that Assange and Manning were accomplices was being rehearsed by prosecutors in the Manning case, beginning at the pre-trial hearing in December 2011, when they revealed the existence of chat logs between Manning and a WikiLeaks interlocutor they claimed to be Assange.[168][169] He "flatly" denies this,[170][171] dismissing the alleged connection as "absolute nonsense."[172] The logs were presented as evidence (Exhibit 123)[173] during the court-martial in June–July 2013. The most significant exchange occurs on the afternoon of 8 March 2010, when Manning asks, "any good at lm hash cracking?", her interlocutor replies, "yes ... we have rainbow tables for lm", Manning sends a string of letters and numbers, and the interlocutor says, "passed it onto our lm guy."[173] The prosecution argued that this shows WikiLeaks helping Manning reverse-engineer a password.[174][175] The evidence that the interlocutor was Assange is circumstantial, however, and Manning insists she acted alone.[160] In January 2011, the U.S. military conceded that it had "been unable to make any direct connection between" the two.[172]

Assange was being examined separately by "several government agencies" in addition to the grand jury.[176] WikiLeaks claims that, "The CIA has publicly declared a WikiLeaks Task Force. Even earlier, the Pentagon publicly declared a 120-member operation into WikiLeaks working 24 hours a day, 7 days a week."[177] An examination of its sources, however, shows that both groups were established to assess and hopefully minimise the impact of the Manning leaks, with the CIA task force looking at the diplomatic cables in the winter of 2010–2011,[178] and the Pentagon operation "combing through" the Iraq war logs the previous autumn, when the concern was "mostly with the threat to individuals, the threat to our people and our equipment."[179] Neither group conducted an investigation "into WikiLeaks." Similarly, the two-year-old Army Counterintelligence Center document published by Assange in 2010 under the headline "U.S. Intelligence planned to destroy WikiLeaks" is a descriptive background paper and "statement of the obvious"[180] containing no "plan" beyond its suggestions that the Army might improve its security training and, if at all possible, catch leakers.[181] The agencies actually taking an interest were less flatteringly cloak-and-dagger, and included the Federal Bureau of Investigation. Prosecutors in the Manning case told another pre-trial hearing in June 2012 that the FBI file on the leaks contained 3,475 documents running to 42,135 pages, only a fifth of which were "germane to Pfc. Manning."[182] The rest were about others, no doubt including Assange. The U.S. government and legislators such as Senator Joe Lieberman also took aim at WikiLeaks itself, using "persuasion and pressure" to deny the organisation technical and financial services from American firms, including Amazon and PayPal.[183] While the government has been criticised for relying on bluff to get results it could not have achieved by court order,[183] it is likely that the firms involved had commercial and fiduciary reasons of their own for breaking with WikiLeaks.[184]

The U.S. Department of Justice confirmed in June 2013 that investigations were "ongoing," although it did not indicate whether they were "active" or merely "open."[176] By November 2013, officials were saying the department had "all but concluded" that it would not bring a case against Assange.[145] The focus of this concession was on the question of publication, and it was less clear whether the department had abandoned hope of prosecuting Assange for conspiring with Manning. That was the implication, but officials declined to say whether they were winding up the grand jury investigation, or even whether there would be an announcement should it be closed.[145]

Assange has never been charged with an offence in the United States, which has also never issued a warrant for his arrest or a request for his extradition. As long as investigations continue, however, uncertainty remains.

Swedish legal position

Demonstration in support of Assange in front of Sydney Town Hall, 10 December 2010
Main article: Assange v Swedish Prosecution Authority

Assange was taken into custody in London on 7 December 2010[185] under a European Arrest Warrant issued on 18 November that year.[186][187] He was released on bail the following week.[188][189]

Assange is wanted for sexual offences—one count of unlawful coercion, two counts of sexual molestation, and one count of rape—allegedly committed against two women during a visit to Sweden in August 2010.[74][190][191] The allegations relate to "non-consensual behaviour within consensual sexual encounters."[192] The apparent ambivalence of the two women, errors in the way Swedish authorities have handled the case, ignorance of Sweden's laws and language,[193] and self-interest fuelled various conspiracy theories,[194] often promoted by Assange's lawyers[195][196][197] and supporters,[194][198][112][199][200] although Assange himself was not blameless.[201][202][203] Prosecutors are seeking to arrest him, finalise charges, and bring him to trial (under Swedish criminal procedure, formal indictment occurs only at the end of an investigation).[190][191] The accusations against Assange "have already been tested in the Swedish legal system," with the Svea Court of Appeal refusing his bid to have the European Arrest Warrant quashed on 24 November 2010.[190]

Sweden's request for his extradition was upheld at hearings before the Westminster Magistrates' Court (February 2011),[204] the High Court (July 2011),[205] and the Supreme Court (February 2012).[206] The Supreme Court delivered its judgement on 30 May 2012 and granted Assange two weeks to make a case for reopening the appeal after his counsel raised fresh concerns.[207] The application was rejected on 14 June,[208] effectively exhausting his legal options in the United Kingdom.[209] The court ruled that the required ten-day "countdown to the final extradition date" should begin after fourteen days.[210] This gave Assange's lawyers until 28 June to interest the European Court of Human Rights at Strasbourg in the case.[210][211]

Political asylum in Ecuador

On 19 June 2012, Ecuadorian Foreign Minister Ricardo Patiño announced that Assange had applied for political asylum, that his government was considering the request, and that Assange was at the Ecuadorian embassy in London.[212][213][214][215][216][217] Patiño's deputy, Kintto Lucas, had canvassed the possibility of Assange taking up residence in Ecuador when he first faced arrest in November 2010,[218][219] but Lucas's views were not endorsed by Patiño[220] or by Ecuadorian President Rafael Correa[221][222] at that time.

Assange said he would go to Sweden if provided with a diplomatic guarantee that he would not be turned over to the U.S.[223] [224] Lawyer David Allen Green responded that while Assange was less likely to be extradited from Sweden than from the United Kingdom, Sweden could not provide such a guarantee under its own or international law: "Assange is asking the impossible, as he probably knows."[190][225] Ecuadorian officials also suggested that Swedish authorities could question Assange at the embassy.[226][227] The prosecutor had already explained that Assange was not wanted "merely to assist with our enquiries," but for "the purpose of conducting criminal proceedings"—that is, to be arrested, charged, and tried.[228][229]

Julian Assange at the Ecuadorian embassy in London

On 16 August 2012, Foreign Minister Patiño announced that Ecuador was granting Assange political asylum.[230][231][232][233] Swedish lawyer Claes Borgström called Ecuador's decision "completely absurd" and "an abuse of the asylum instrument,"[234] a view echoed by others.[153][235][236] Latin American states expressed support for Ecuador.[237][238][239][240] President Correa confirmed on 18 August that Assange could stay at the embassy indefinitely,[241][242][243] and the following day he gave the first of his addresses from the balcony.[244][245][246][247]

His home since then has been an office converted into a living room, equipped with a bed, telephone, sun lamp, computer, shower, treadmill, and kitchenette.[248][249][250][251][252] While speculation that British authorities would try to extricate Assange from the embassy by force proved baseless,[253][254][255][256][257][258] officers of the Metropolitan Police Service remain stationed outside the building to arrest him should he try to leave. The cost of this operation to 31 January 2013 (less than eight months) was £2.9 million (US$4.5 million).[259]

Also out of pocket were the individuals who stood bail for Assange. The "celebrity backers" (Ken Loach, Jemima Khan, Michael Moore, John Pilger, and others) who had provided a cash deposit of £200,000 apparently forfeited everything in July 2012,[260][261][262][110] and in October, a separate group of nine supporters who had pledged sureties totalling £140,000 were ordered to pay £93,500 after the judge made various reductions on compassionate grounds.[260][263][264][265][266]

Impasse

Google Trends suggests that interest in Assange spiked at the time of his arrest in December 2010 (index=100) and has flatlined since then (average index=6 to February 2014), save for a resurgence when he was granted asylum in August 2012 (index=34).[267]

Assange was still visible in 2012, with an appearance on The Simpsons in February,[268][269] the announcement that he would run for the Australian Senate in March,[270][271] his own talk show on Russia Today in April–July, a much-publicised visit by Lady Gaga to the Ecuadorian embassy in October,[272] an apparent nod from the James Bond film Skyfall released later the same month, and the publication of Cypherpunks[56] in November. It was debatable, however, what any of this had to do with WikiLeaks, and the longer he remained in physical isolation, the more his currency and influence diminished.[16][273] This was reflected in the mixed fortunes of the film treatments that appeared belatedly in 2012 and 2013. The telemovie Underground: The Julian Assange Story (2012) won its timeslot when it premiered on Australian free-to-air,[274] the documentary We Steal Secrets: The Story of WikiLeaks (2013) attracted favourable reviews but modest audiences,[275] and the thriller The Fifth Estate (2013) inspired neither critics or moviegoers, ranking as the year's "top turkey."[276] While Assange opposed The Fifth Estate and welcomed its failure,[277][278] there was no escaping the suspicion that "the subject matter ... was its biggest weakness."[279]

Far and away Assange's greatest non-legal problem has been the dearth of compelling new leaks since 2010; Chelsea Manning was a hard act to follow.[77] He has resorted to republishing historic records freely available at the U.S. National Archives.[280][281] Nor has he been helped by the alienation of friends and collaborators, from Daniel Domscheit-Berg[282] to Jemima Khan.[108] Even supporters called him a "jerk,"[283] and lack of success made people less forgiving of his temperament.[16] The character question had in fact dogged Assange for some time, particularly since his ill-judged response[284][285] to the publication of leaked Swedish police documents detailing his alleged offences.[74] His complaints about the impropriety of the leak and the invasion of his privacy[284][285] were greeted with predictable derision,[286][287] even from liberal commentators,[288] as were his habit of referring to himself in the third person,[285][108] and his estimate that "50 per cent of people" would count him and Martin Luther King as their "only ... two heroes in the world."[289][290] He already seemed "a forgotten man"[176] when the eruption of Edward Snowden in June 2013 cast him (and WikiLeaks) into near-total eclipse.[291] (Despite his role in getting Snowden from Hong Kong to Moscow.)[273] The publication of a scathing portrait by his ghostwriter Andrew O'Hagan in March 2014[16] prompted some to dismiss him as a spent force.[292] Others have countered that this is premature—Assange remains active, has many supporters, and is still only in middle age.[293]

Writings

Assange's autonomous literary output amounts to a few short pieces, including "State and terrorist conspiracies" (2006),[294] "Conspiracy as governance" (2006),[295] "The hidden curse of Thomas Paine" (2008),[296] "What’s new about WikiLeaks?" (2011),[297] and the foreword to Cypherpunks (2012).[56] He also contributed research to Suelette Dreyfus's Underground (1997),[30] and received a co-writer credit for the Calle 13 song "Multi_Viral" (2013).

He is yet to produce the "manifesto" or "moral essays" he believes might do justice to his ideas.[16] (Namely, hostility to superpowers, especially the United States, and support for countries that resist them, information transparency, and the "market libertarianism"[298] espoused by the Ron Paul wing of the Republican Party.) His Unauthorised Autobiography (2011) appears to be entirely the work of Andrew O'Hagan, with Assange contributing nothing beyond the initial interviews.[16] Cypherpunks is also recycled interview material, much of it supplied by Jacob Appelbaum, Andy Müller-Maguhn, and Jérémie Zimmermann.[299] O'Hagan suggests that Assange is not a natural writer.[16]

Personal life

While still in his teens, Assange married a woman known only as Teresa, and in 1989 they had a son, Daniel Assange, now a software designer.[22][29][300] The couple separated and fought over custody of the child until 1999.[9] Assange says he was Daniel's primary caregiver for much of his childhood.[301] Assange is reported to have fathered other offspring, including a daughter in 2006, and has apparently referred to "my daughter"[302] and "my children,"[303] but the evidence on this is confused.[16][22][142][304] For a time he was the partner of WikiLeaks journalist Sarah Harrison.[16]
