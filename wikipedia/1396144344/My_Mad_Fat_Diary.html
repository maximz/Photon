My Mad Fat Diary is a British comedy-drama television series that debuted on E4 on 14 January 2013. It is based on "My Fat, Mad Teenage Diary" written by Rae Earl.[1]

E4 have confirmed that the show had been renewed for a second series.[2] The second series started on 17 February 2014.

Plot[edit]

Set in Stamford, Lincolnshire in 1996, My Mad Fat Diary follows the story of 16-year-old, 16 stone girl, Rae Earl, who has just left a psychiatric hospital, where she has spent four months.[3] She begins to reconnect with her best friend, Chloe, who is unaware of Rae's mental health and body image problems, believing she was in France for the past four months. In series two, we discover that this isn't true, however, but that Chloe's parents tell Chloe where Rae has really been the past four months. Before this if fact is revealed to the viewers in episode 2.06, we are under the impression that Chloe is unaware of Rae's mental illness and stay at the psychiatric hospital until Rae tells Chloe herself. Rae attempts to keep this information from her while also trying to impress Chloe's friends Izzy, Archie, Chop and Finn.[4]

Cast and characters[edit]

Main cast[edit]

  • Sharon Rooney as Rachel 'Rae' Earl, an overweight 16-year-old who has spent the past four months in a psychiatric hospital. She struggles to hide her mental health and body image problems from her new friends and finds it hard to fit in.
  • Ian Hart as Kester, Rae's therapist.
  • Claire Rushbrook as Linda Bouchtat,[5] Rae's mum.
  • Jodie Comer as Chloe Gemell, Rae's attractive, popular best friend.
  • Nico Mirallegro as Finn Nelson, a boy in Rae's new social group whom she clashes with and who later becomes her love interest.
  • Dan Cohen as Archie, a boy in Rae's social group she is friends with, later revealed to be gay, but closeted.
  • Jordan Murphy as Chop, the party animal of the group. In the series one finale, it is revealed that his real name is Arnold Peters.[6] He tells Izzy he loves her in the series one finale and they begin a relationship.
  • Ciara Baxendale as Izzy, the sweet but ditzy girl in the group. She's dating Chop.
  • Sophie Wright as Tix, a resident of the psychiatric hospital and the friend Rae confides in.

Recurring cast[edit]

  • Bamshad Abedi-Amin as Karim Bouchtat, the undocumented immigrant husband of Rae's mum.
  • Shazad Latif as Dr. Nick Kassar, a doctor at the psychiatric hospital.
  • Darren Evans as Danny Two Hats, a resident of the psychiatric hospital.
  • Sacha Parkinson as Stacey, a popular girl who Rae becomes friends with.
  • Turlough Convery as Liam Owen, Rae's new friend who attends the group therapy.
  • Eliot Otis Brown Walters as Big G, a bully who often picks on Rae, calling her Jabba.

Series Two[edit]

The show, which drew an average of 1.2 million viewers per episode, has been re-commissioned for a second series[14] and it started filming in June/July 2013. It aired on 17 February 2014.[15] The second season will continue to air on E4, and will consist of seven episodes.[2] The series will feature Rae starting college.

Critical response[edit]

My Mad Fat Diary has received critical acclaim. The Guardian's Sam Wollaston called it a "lovely drama – honest and painful, real, and very funny," going on to say "Sharon Rooney's performance in the lead is natural, effortless and utterly believable; she should win something for it."[23] The Stage called it "a comedy drama that actually satisfies the criteria of both genres, My Mad Fat Diary offers a unique and uncompromising perspective on adolescent angst that distresses and delights by turns. Visual gimmicks – flashbacks, fantasies and animated squiggles leaping from the page – are used sparingly but effectively, allowing the focus to stay fixed on Rae and Rooney’s commanding and engagingly natural central performance."[24] The Art Desk said "the first episode of this six-part comedy drama is touching, hilarious and perfectly cast." Claire Webb of the Radio Times said the plot is "as uplifting as it is moving, although the banter and gimmicks won't be to everybody's taste."[25] American radio station WBEZ in Chicago, Illinois, called the series "The best television show you aren't watching."[26]

The show also received praise for its honest portrayal of mental health. Brian Semple of "The Independent" calls the show "surprisingly honest, funny and even moving account of what it’s like for a teenage girl to live with serious mental health problems, free of many of the clichés that often inform how mental illness is portrayed on TV," going on to say that Rae "has a mental illness, but it doesn’t define her. It’s just something that she has to deal with and try to manage on a daily basis, just like the one in ten young people in the UK who have a mental illness." Semple refers to My Mad Fat Diary as a "breath of fresh air and will do a lot to change the way young people think about mental health.".[27]

In a more mixed review, Robert Epstein of The Independent criticised E4 for relating the programme to its other teen shows: "If such comparisons are unfair, blame it on E4, whose continuity announcer declared: 'If you like Skins, The Inbetweeners and Misfits, you'll like this.' Well, sorry, I do like those three shows, but, even with a great soundtrack (if only the Mack really would return …) and the odd nice line, My Mad Fat Diary is a long way from the equal of that trio in invention, edge or humour."[28]

My Mad Fat Diary star Sharon Rooney was chosen as one of the first group of BAFTA "Breakthrough Brits" in 2013.[29]
