Sim Bhullar (born December 2, 1992) is a 7-foot-5-inch[1] Indo-Canadian college basketball player at New Mexico State University. The New York Times stated he "is poised to become the world's first prominent men's basketball player of Indian and Sikh descent."[2]

Bio[edit]

Bhullar is a native of Toronto, Canada. His parents migrated to Canada from the Punjab. His father, Avtar, is 6 feet 5 inches (196 cm) and his mother, Varinder, is 5 feet 10 inches (178 cm). Sim's younger brother, Tanveer, who stands at 7 feet 3 inches (221 cm), is also a promising basketball prospect. Neither parent had exposure to basketball until enrolling Sim and Tanveer into local youth basketball programs. Avtar himself grew up only playing Kabaddi, a traditional Punjab contact sport. Avtar had initially wanted his sons to play cricket, but soon realized that the boys height would not allow for them to move swiftly on the field. [3]

High school career[edit]

To better develop their basketball skills, Sim and his younger brother Tanveer moved from Toronto and enrolled in the Kiski School in Saltsburg, Pennsylvania, U.S. for the 2009–10 school year.[4] That year, Bhullar averaged a triple-double for the Kiski basketball team with 26 points, 24 rebounds and 11 blocks. At the FIBA Americas U18 tournament in the summer of 2010, Sim impressed with his size and performance. In a loss to the U.S. team, Bhullar came off the bench to score 28 points, grab 31 rebounds and block 13 shots.[3]

At the end of November 2010, in the middle of Kiski's basketball season, Sim and Tanveer withdrew from Kiski.[4] Sim transferred to Huntington Prep School in West Virginia.[5] At Huntington, Sim improved on his conditioning, dropping from 367 pounds to 330.[2]

College career[edit]

Bhullar originally committed to playing for Xavier University in Cincinnati, Ohio but decommitted in August 2011 to play for the New Mexico State Aggies. He turned down Xavier because he learned he was not qualified to immediately play and would have to pay the full $42,000 a year tuition, which his family was not prepared to do.[6] Bhullar, however, was not able to play until the 2012-2013 season because the NCAA denied him an eligibility waiver and rejected the school's appeal.[7] His brother, Tanveer, joined the team for the 2013-14 season, although he redshirted his freshman year.

Impact[edit]

In a July 2011 profile of Bhullar, The New York Times stated he "is poised to become the world's first prominent men's basketball player of Indian descent" and could be the "next great basketball ambassador" to Asia since Chinese star Yao Ming.[2]
