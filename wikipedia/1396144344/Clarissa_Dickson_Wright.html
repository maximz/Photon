Clarissa Dickson Wright (24 June 1947 – 15 March 2014) was an English celebrity chef, television personality, writer, businesswoman and formerly a barrister (the youngest person to be called to the Bar at the time[1]), who was best known as one half, along with Jennifer Paterson, of the Two Fat Ladies television cooking programme. She was an accredited cricket umpire, and one of only two women to become a Guild Butcher.

Early life[edit]

Clarissa Theresa Philomena Aileen Mary Josephine Agnes Elsie Trilby Louise Esmerelda Dickson Wright was born in 1947 in St John's Wood, London,[2] the youngest of four children.[3] Her father, Arthur Dickson Wright, was a surgeon to the Royal Family, and her mother, Molly (née Bath),[2] was an Australian heiress.[1] Dickson Wright's father was an alcoholic who subjected his wife and children to verbal and physical abuse.[4]

At the age of 11 Wright was sent to Sacred Heart School. After school Clarissa studied for the Bar at Gray's Inn, while pursuing a law degree at University College London.[5]

Later life[edit]

At the age of 21, Dickson Wright passed her Bar exams and became England's youngest barrister.[1] After her mother died of a heart attack in 1975, she inherited £2.8 million. Her mother's death, combined a few years later with her father's, left her in a deep depression, and she drank heavily for the following 12 years.[5]

In 1979, Clarissa Dickson Wright took control of the food at a drinking club in St James's Place in London. While there she met Clive ("no surname, because he has children" according to Dickson Wright), a fellow alcoholic, and they had a relationship until his death in 1982 from kidney failure at the age of 40.[1] Shortly thereafter she was disbarred for practising without chambers. [6]Dickson Wright claimed that, during her alcoholic years, she had sex with an MP behind the Speaker's chair in the House of Commons.[1]

In the early 1980s she was homeless and staying with friends.[7] For two years she was cook-housekeeper for a family in Sussex until she was fired for her alcohol-induced behaviour. [8]After being charged with driving under the influence, Dickson Wright started to attend Alcoholics Anonymous meetings, counselling, and a detox centre.[1] She attended the Promis Recovery Centre at Nonington. [9] In her 2009 book Rifling through my Drawers she expressed a belief in reincarnation. She was a keen supporter of hunting. [10][11]

Cooking and television career[edit]

Seven months after leaving Promis, Dickson Wright offered to run Books For Cooks, a shop and cafe in Portobello Road, London, for the shop's owner.[12] After seven years, the owner decided to sell the shop; it was offered to Dickson Wright, but she did not have the money, and was sacked by the owner.[12] She then moved to Edinburgh and ran the Cooks Book Shop.[12]

During her time in Edinburgh, television producer Patricia Llewellyn asked her and Jennifer Paterson if they wanted to make a television programme; they made a pilot in 1994.[12] After the pilot, BBC2 commissioned a series of Two Fat Ladies. Three successful series were made and shown around the world.[12] Paterson died in 1999 mid-way through the fourth series.[13]

Last years[edit]

Two Fat Ladies ended after Paterson's death. Dickson Wright appeared with Johnny Scott in Clarissa and the Countryman from 2000 to 2003 and played the gamekeeper in the sitcom Absolutely Fabulous in 2003.[5] In 2005, Dickson Wright took part in the BBC reality television show Art School.

Dickson Wright campaigned for the Countryside Alliance and was the first female Rector of the University of Aberdeen.[5] Her autobiography, Spilling The Beans, was published in September 2007. In 2008, she presented a one-off documentary for BBC Four, Clarissa and the King's Cookbook, where she makes recipes from a cookbook dating to the reign of Richard II.[14]

Along with racehorse trainer Sir Mark Prescott, Dickson Wright was charged with hare coursing with dogs in North Yorkshire in March 2007 under a private prosecution lodged by the International Fund for Animal Welfare under the Hunting Act 2004.[15][16][17] On 1 September 2009, she and Prescott pleaded guilty and received an absolute discharge at Scarborough Magistrates' Court. They said that they were invited to the event by the Yorkshire Greyhound Field Trialling Club, which told the court that it believed it was running a legal event by using muzzled dogs.[16]

In October 2012, Dickson Wright appeared on Fieldsports Britain to discuss badgers and their nutritional value, saying: "There's going to be a cull, so rather than just throw them in the landfill site, why not eat them?"[18] In November 2012, she presented a short BBC4 TV series on the history of the British breakfast, lunch and dinner.

She lived in Inveresk, near Edinburgh.[19] She had been in hospital since the start of 2014 and died in the Edinburgh Royal Infirmary on 15 March 2014.[20][21]
