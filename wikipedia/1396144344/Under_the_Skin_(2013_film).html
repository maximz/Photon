Under the Skin is a 2013 British-American[1] science fiction film directed by Jonathan Glazer. Produced by James Wilson and Nick Wechsler, it was written by Glazer and Walter Campbell as an adaption of Michel Faber's 2000 novel of the same name. The film stars Scarlett Johansson as an alien who preys on men in Scotland.

Plot[edit]

In Scotland, an alien takes the body of an attractive young woman and travels the country in a van picking up men. As she lures her victims into sex, the men are trapped in a kind of preserving liquid and their organs are harvested. She is monitored by another alien, who has the body of a male motorcyclist.

When she attempts to seduce a man on a beach, she witnesses the accidental drowning of a man and woman. She watches dispassionately, then kills the man after he tries to save them. She takes his body with her, leaving the couple's toddler behind.

When she seduces a lonely and sexually inexperienced man who has neurofibromatosis disfigurement, she takes pity on him and allows him to escape. The man is tracked and killed by the motorcyclist, who sets out in pursuit of the alien woman across Scotland.

Troubled by the encounter with the man with neurofibromatosis, the alien undergoes an identity crisis, and begins to feel more conscious of her human body; she becomes depressed and eventually catatonic. She is helped by a man who takes her to his home, cooks for her, and gives her her own bed. She trusts him and they attempt to make love, but are unable to.

Confused, the alien goes for a walk in a deep forest and takes shelter in a cabin. She is woken by a forest ranger, who tries to rape her; in the process, he tears her skin, revealing her alien body. As she extricates herself from her human skin, the ranger douses her in petrol and burns her alive. The smoke from her burnt body coils into the sky.

Production[edit]

Many of the scenes where Johansson's character picks up men were unscripted conversations with non-actors, filmed with hidden cameras. Director Jonathan Glazer said the men were "talked through what extremes they would have to go to if they agreed to take part in the film once they understood what we were doing."[2]

Release[edit]

Under the Skin premiered on 29 August 2013 at the Telluride Film Festival. It later played in competition at the 70th Venice International Film Festival. The film was also screened at the 2013 Toronto International Film Festival.[3][4] It was released in the United Kingdom on 14 March 2014[5] and will be released in the United States on 4 April 2014.[6]

Reception[edit]

Box office[edit]

Under the Skin opened with a gross of £239,000 at the UK box office.[7]

Critical response[edit]

Under the Skin received very positive reviews. The review aggregation website Rotten Tomatoes gives the film a score of 89% based on reviews from 57 critics, with an average score of 8.4/10. The site's consensus states: "Its message may prove elusive for some, but with absorbing imagery and a mesmerizing performance from Scarlett Johansson, Under the Skin is a haunting viewing experience."[8] Another review aggregator, Metacritic, which assigns a normalized rating out of 100 top reviews from mainstream critics, calculated a score of 80 out of 100 based on 11 reviews, signifying "generally positive reviews".[9]

Xan Brooks of The Guardian gave Under the Skin five out of five and called it "far and away the best picture" in the Venice Film Festival.[10] Peter Bradshaw, also of The Guardian, said it was "visually stunning and deeply disturbing",[11] and gave the film a perfect score of five stars, along with Andrew Lowry of Total Film,[12] Dave Calhoun of Time Out (magazine),[13] and Kate Muir of The Times,[14] Robbie Collin of The Daily Telegraph also gave it five stars, and wrote: "if my legs hadn't been so wobbly and my mouth so dry, I would have climbed up on my seat and cheered."[15]

Kaleem Aftab of The Independent gave the film one star out of five, calling it "laughably bad".[16] Henry Fitzherbert of The Daily Express wrote "it didn't get under my skin, just on my nerves", awarding it two out of five.[17] Todd McCarthy of The Hollywood Reporter said that "the film provides too little for even relatively adventurous specialized audiences to latch onto."[18]
