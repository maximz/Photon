Michael Christopher Sheen, OBE (born 5 February 1969),[1] is a Welsh stage and screen actor. After training at London's Royal Academy of Dramatic Art, Sheen made his professional debut in 1991, starring opposite Vanessa Redgrave in When She Danced at the Globe Theatre. He worked mainly in theatre throughout the 1990s and made notable stage appearances in Romeo and Juliet (1992), Don’t Fool With Love (1993), Peer Gynt (1994), The Seagull (1995), The Homecoming (1997) and Henry V (1997). His performances in Amadeus at the Old Vic and Look Back in Anger at the National Theatre were nominated for Olivier Awards in 1998 and 1999, respectively. In 2003, he was nominated for a third Olivier Award for his performance in Caligula at the Donmar Warehouse.

Sheen has become better known as a screen actor since the 2000s, in particular through his roles in various biopics.[2] With writer Peter Morgan he has starred in a trilogy of films as British politician Tony Blair: the first was the television film The Deal in 2003, followed by The Queen (2006) and The Special Relationship (2010). For playing Blair he was nominated for both a BAFTA Award and an Emmy. Sheen was also nominated for a BAFTA as the troubled comic actor Kenneth Williams in BBC Four's 2006 Fantabulosa!, and was nominated for a fourth Olivier Award nomination in 2006 for portraying the broadcaster David Frost in Frost/Nixon, a role he revisited in the 2008 film adaptation of the play. He also starred as the outspoken football manager Brian Clough in The Damned United.

Since 2009 and into the 2010s, Sheen has become known for a wider variety of roles. In 2009, Sheen appeared in two fantasy films, Underworld: Rise of the Lycans and The Twilight Saga: New Moon, while in 2010, he made a four-episode guest appearance in the NBC comedy 30 Rock. He appeared in the science-fiction film Tron: Legacy (2010) and Woody Allen's romantic comedy Midnight in Paris (2011). At Easter 2011, Sheen directed and starred in National Theatre Wales's The Passion, a 72-hour secular passion play staged in his hometown of Port Talbot. From late 2011 until early 2012, Sheen played the title role in Hamlet at the Young Vic. In 2013, he received a Golden Globe nomination for his role in Showtime's television drama Masters of Sex.

Early life[edit]

Sheen was born in Newport, Wales,[3] the son of Irene (née Thomas), a secretary, and Meyrick, a British Steel personnel manager.[4][5] He has one younger sister, Joanne.[5] When he was five, the family moved to Liverpool, England, but settled in his parents' hometown of Baglan in Port Talbot, Wales three years later.[6][7] Port Talbot is also the hometown of actors Richard Burton and Anthony Hopkins. Director Sam Mendes has described Sheen as "a stage creature" and attributed that to the actor's Welsh roots. "I'm serious. He's Welsh in the tradition of Anthony Hopkins and Richard Burton: fiery, mercurial, unpredictable."[8] A keen footballer, Sheen was scouted and offered a place on Arsenal F.C.'s youth team at the age of 12 but his family was unwilling to relocate to London.[7] He later said he was "grateful" for his parents' decision as the chances of forging a professional football career were "so slim". "It would have meant moving from Wales to London as a family. It was just too big an upheaval."[9] He lost interest in playing football in his early teens.[7]

Sheen was raised in a theatrical family—his parents were both involved in local amateur operatics and musicals[10] and, later in life, his father worked as a part-time professional Jack Nicholson look-alike.[11] In his teenage years, Sheen was involved with the West Glamorgan Youth Theatre and, later, the National Youth Theatre of Wales.[10][12] "It was a brilliant youth theatre", Sheen has said, "and it taught me not only a lot about acting, but also about work ethic; it was very disciplined."[13] He was influenced by the performances of Laurence Olivier and the writings of theatre critic Kenneth Tynan—"the combination of those two things kind of blew my head off."[14][15] Sheen was educated at Blaenbaglan Primary School, Glan Afan Comprehensive School and, finally, Neath Port Talbot College where he sat A-levels in English, Drama and Sociology.[5] He considered studying English at university but instead decided to attend drama school.[10] He moved to London in 1988 to train as an actor at the Royal Academy of Dramatic Art (RADA),[10] having spent the previous year working in a Welsh fast food restaurant called Burger Master to earn money.[5] Sheen was granted the Laurence Olivier Bursary by The Society of London Theatre in his second year at RADA.[16][17] He graduated in 1991 with a BA in Acting.[5]

Career[edit]

Classical stage roles (1991–2001)[edit]

Sheen worked predominantly in theatre in the 1990s and has since remarked that he will always feel "slightly more at home" on stage. "It's more of an actor's medium. You are your own editor, nobody else is choosing what is being seen of you."[18] His first professional role, while still in his third and final year at RADA, was in When She Danced at the Globe Theatre in 1991.[19] He later described the role as "a big break. One day I was at RADA doing a movement class, the next I was at a read-through with Vanessa Redgrave and Frances de la Tour."[20] Milton Shulman of the Evening Standard praised an "excellent" performance[21] while The Observer wrote of "a notable West End debut".[22] In 1992, Sheen's performance in Romeo and Juliet at the Royal Exchange received a MEN Theatre Award nomination[23] and led theatre critic Michael Coveney to declare him "the most exciting young actor of his generation ... a volatile, electrifying and technically fearless performer".[11][24] His 1993 turn as Perdican in Alfred de Musset's Don’t Fool With Love at the Donmar Warehouse was nominated for the Ian Charleson Award[25] and was described by The Independent as "quite thrilling".[26] Also in 1993, Sheen appeared in the world premiere of Harold Pinter's Moonlight at the Almeida Theatre[27] and made his television debut in the 1993 BBC mini-series Gallowglass.[28]

Sheen played the title role in Peer Gynt in 1994. The Yukio Ninagawa production was staged in Oslo, Tokyo and at the Barbican Centre, London. The Times praised Sheen's "astonishing vitality"[29] while The Independent found him "sensationally good" and noted that "the Norwegian press were grudgingly captivated by the mercurial Welsh boyo".[30] In other 1994 work, Sheen appeared in Le Livre de Spencer at the Odéon-Théâtre de l'Europe, Paris[31] and starred in the cross-dressing farce Charley's Aunt at the Royal Exchange.[32] In 1995, he appeared opposite Kate Beckinsale in a production of The Seagull at the Theatre Royal, Bath[33] and, with the encouragement of Thelma Holt,[34] directed and starred in The Dresser at the Theatre Royal, Plymouth. In addition, Sheen made his film debut that year, appearing opposite Kenneth Branagh in Othello.[35] 1996 saw Sheen at the National Theatre for The Ends of the Earth, an original play by David Lan.[36] A minor role in Mary Reilly marked the first of three film collaborations with director Stephen Frears.[37] Sheen's most significant appearance of 1997 was the title role in Henry V, staged by the Royal Shakespeare Company (RSC) at their Stratford-upon-Avon theatre, which earned him a second Ian Charleson Award nomination.[38] The Times praised "a blisteringly intelligent performance".[39] Also in 1997, he appeared in a revival of Harold Pinter's The Homecoming at the National Theatre, directed by Roger Michell,[40] and directed Badfinger, starring Rhys Ifans, at the Donmar Warehouse.[41][42][43] The latter was staged by the Thin Language Theatre Company, which Sheen had co-founded in 1991, aiming to further Welsh theatre.[34][44] He then appeared in the biographical film Wilde, playing Robbie Ross to Stephen Fry's Oscar Wilde.[45] In early 1998 Sheen formed a production company, The Foundry, with Helen McCrory and Robert Delamere to promote the work of emerging playwrights,[44] and produced A Little World of Our Own at the Donmar Warehouse, which gave Colin Farrell his West End debut.[46]

The Old Vic, where Sheen starred in a successful revival of Amadeus in 1998. The play later transferred to Broadway.

From 1998 to 1999, Sheen starred as Mozart in a successful revival of Amadeus.[47] The Peter Hall-directed production was staged at the Old Vic, London and later transferred to the Music Box on Broadway. Ben Brantley, chief theatre critic for The New York Times, was particularly vocal in his praise. He noted that "Mr. Sheen elicits a real poetry from the role" and felt that, while watching him, "you start to appreciate the derivation of the term star. This actor is so luminous it's scary!"[48] The Independent found him "quite stunning as Mozart. His fantastically physical performance convinces you of his character's genius and the play catches fire whenever he's on stage."[49] Sheen was nominated for a Laurence Olivier Award for Best Supporting Performance and an Outer Critics Circle Award for Outstanding Actor.[25][50][51] In 1999, Sheen explored the role of Jimmy Porter in the National Theatre's production of Look Back in Anger. In 2003, Sheen described the production as "the most enjoyable thing I've ever done ... everything came together".[52] The Daily Mail declared him a "definitive Jimmy Porter... simply exhilarating in his great jazz riffs of speeches, mercurial and irresistible."[53] "Sheen has cornered the market in explosive energy", said The Independent, "but this thrilling performance is his finest yet."[54] The Financial Times noted: "As Jimmy Porter, a role of staggering difficulty in every way, Michael Sheen gives surely the best performance London has yet seen from him ... You hang on every word he utters ... This is a dazzlingly through-the-body performance."[55] He was nominated for a Laurence Olivier Award for Best Actor and an Evening Standard Award for Best Actor.[56]

The Deal, The Queen and Fantabulosa (2002–2006)[edit]

At this point in his career, Sheen began to devote more time to film work.[57] Heartlands, a little-seen 2002 film about a naive man's road trip in the Midlands, was his first leading film role.[58] While The Guardian dismissed the "cloying bittersweet-regional-lottery-Britfilm", it noted that "Sheen himself has a childlike, Frank Spencer-ish charm".[59] "It was great to do something that was so different", Sheen has said of the role. "I usually play very extreme characters but I couldn't get away with doing all my usual silly tricks with Colin."[60] Also in 2002, he had a minor role in the action-adventure film The Four Feathers.[61] In 2003, Sheen appeared in Bright Young Things, the directorial debut of his Wilde co-star, Stephen Fry. An adaptation of Evelyn Waugh's novel, the film followed high society partygoers in decadent, pre-war London. Sheen played a gay aristocrat in an ensemble cast which included James McAvoy, Emily Mortimer, David Tennant, Dan Aykroyd, Jim Broadbent and Peter O'Toole. While the Los Angeles Times said he "shone",[62] The Guardian felt the role "drastically under-uses his talents".[63] Sheen described his character as "possibly the campest man in cinema history" and relished a scene "where I do drugs with [a then 95-year-old] Sir John Mills."[64] In other 2003 film work, Sheen appeared as a werewolf named Lucian in Underworld[65] and made a brief appearance in the sci-fi film Timeline.[66]

Sheen also returned to the stage in 2003 to play the title role in Caligula at the Donmar Warehouse, directed by Michael Grandage. It was the first of just three stage appearances during the 2000s; his young daughter was now based in Los Angeles, California which made more frequent stage runs in Britain impractical.[67] The Independent's critic declared it "one of the most thrilling and searching performances I have ever witnessed"[68] while The Daily Telegraph described him as an "outrageously charismatic actor" with "an astonishing physical presence".[69] The Times praised a "riveting performance"[70] while The Guardian found him "highly impressive ... at one point he attacks his court poet with a single hair-raising leap across a chair and table".[71] Sheen won an Evening Standard Award for Best Actor and a Critics' Circle Theatre Award for Best Actor, and was again nominated for a Laurence Olivier Award for Best Actor.[72][73]

Sheen's breakthrough role was as British politician Tony Blair in 2003's The Deal. The Channel 4 film explored the so-called Granita pact made by Tony Blair and Gordon Brown prior to the 1994 Labour Party leadership election, and was the actor's first collaboration with screenwriter Peter Morgan. Director Stephen Frears cast him because "he was in Mary Reilly and I knew he was brilliant."[74] Filmed while he was playing Caligula nightly on stage, Sheen has remarked, "It’s interesting that in searching for monsters to play, you often end up playing leaders."[75] The Daily Telegraph praised his "earnest, yet steely, portrayal"[76] while The Guardian found him "excellent. This is intelligent and honest casting."[77] In 2004, Sheen starred in ITV's Dirty Filthy Love, a comic film about a man dealing with OCD and Tourette's after a marital separation. Sheen spoke of "treading a fine line" because "a lot of the symptoms are intrinsically comical".[72] He was nominated for a BAFTA Award for Best Television Actor and a RTS Best Actor Award.[78][79] Also in 2004, Sheen played a pompous rock star in the romantic comedy Laws of Attraction[80] and produced and starred in The Banker, which won a BAFTA Award for Best Short Film.[81][82] In 2005, Sheen starred in the National Theatre's production of The UN Inspector, a David Farr adaptation of The Government Inspector. The Times wrote of "a scathingly brilliant and inventive performance"[83] while Variety noted that the actor "adds comic finesse to his apparently ceaseless repertoire".[84] The Evening Standard, while conceding that the performance was "technically brilliant", expressed bemusement as to why "one of the most mercurial and inspiring actors we have seems set on impersonating Rik Mayall throughout".[85] Also that year, he took part in the Old Vic's 24 Hour Play,[86] in which The Telegraph felt he "dazzled".[87] In 2005 film work, Sheen starred in Dead Long Enough, a small-budget Welsh/Irish film, with his longtime friend, Jason Hughes.[88][89] In addition, he had a supporting role in Ridley Scott's Kingdom of Heaven,[90] made a cameo appearance in The League of Gentlemen's Apocalypse[91] and starred in the short film The Open Doors.[92]

Sheen came to international attention in 2006 for his portrayal of Tony Blair in The Queen. The film focused on the differing reactions of the British Royal Family and the newly appointed Prime Minister following the death of Diana, Princess of Wales in 1997; it was Sheen's third collaboration with director Stephen Frears and his second with screenwriter Peter Morgan. He enjoyed reprising his role because Blair, at this point in his career, had "a weight to him that he didn’t have before".[15] When asked to discuss his personal opinion of Blair, Sheen admitted that the more time he spent working on the character, the "less opinion" he has of the politician: "Now when I watch him on TV or hear his voice, it's sort of like a cross between a family member, a friend and seeing a really old embarrassing video of yourself."[93] Peter Travers of Rolling Stone praised "a sensational performance, alert and nuanced"[94] while Empire spoke of an "uncanny, insightful performance".[95] Sheen was nominated for a BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role.[96] His second film appearance of 2006 was a supporting role in Blood Diamond as an unscrupulous diamond dealer.[97] Also in 2006, Sheen starred as the troubled English comic actor Kenneth Williams in BBC Four's Fantabulosa! In preparation for the role, he lost two and a half stone (approx. 35 lbs), studied archival footage and read Williams' published diaries.[15] Sheen has said he is "fascinated by finding the private side of the public face".[98] The Times found his performance "mesmerising"[99] while The Observer described it as "a characterisation for which the description tour-de-force is, frankly, pretty faint praise".[100] He won a RTS Award for Best Actor,[101] and received his second BAFTA nomination of 2006, for Best Television Actor.[102] Sheen starred in two other BBC television productions in 2006, playing H. G. Wells in HG Wells: War with the World[103] and Nero in Ancient Rome: The Rise and Fall of an Empire.[104]

Frost/Nixon and The Damned United (2007–2009)[edit]

From 2006 to 2007, Sheen starred as the television broadcaster David Frost in Frost/Nixon at both the Donmar Warehouse and Gielgud Theatre in London and at the Jacobs Theatre on Broadway. The play, written by Peter Morgan, directed by Michael Grandage and co-starring Frank Langella, was a critical and commercial success[105] but Sheen initially accepted the role as a favour to his friends and "never thought it was going anywhere".[106] The Guardian said the actor "exactly captures Frost's verbal tics and mannerisms while suggesting a nervousness behind the self-assurance".[107] "He’s got the voice, the mannerisms, the blaze," said the Financial Times, "but, more than that, Sheen—as viscerally exciting an actor as any in Britain today—shows us the hunger of Frost’s ambition .. and fox-like instinct for the hunt and the kill."[108] Sheen was nominated for a Laurence Olivier Award for Best Actor and a Drama League Award for Distinguished Performance.[109][110] Sheen next appeared in the 2007 film Music Within as a political activist with cerebral palsy. He spoke of having a "responsibility" to accurately portray the condition.[111] Variety said his performance was "remarkable.. utterly convincing",[112] USA Today found him "outstanding"[113] while the Los Angeles Times felt he was "reminiscent of Daniel Day-Lewis in My Left Foot, bringing a vibrancy and wit to the role".[114] Also that year, Sheen starred in the short film Airlock, or How To Say Goodbye in Space with Derek Jacobi[115] and was invited to join the actors' branch of the Academy of Motion Picture Arts & Sciences.[116]

Sheen reprised the role of David Frost in 2008's Frost/Nixon, a film dramatisation of the The Nixon Interviews of 1977. Despite appearing in the original stage production in a part written for him by Peter Morgan, Sheen was surprised to have been cast in the film: "Peter said he'd only be prepared to give the rights to someone who would cast me as Frost, which was very nice, but when the studios get their hands on something... Right up until we started filming I was prepared to be disappointed".[117] Roger Ebert of the Chicago Sun-Times asserted that Sheen embodied his character in a "compelling, intense" performance[118] while The Wall Street Journal felt he was "a brilliant actor" who "grows his character from a bright-eyed social butterfly to a gimlet-eyed interrogator".[119] However, The New York Times felt "the likable, watchable Mr. Sheen has been pitted against a scene-stealer" in Frank Langella's Nixon.[120] Frost himself later said it was "a wonderful performance".[121] Sheen was the recipient of the Variety Award at the British Independent Film Awards 2008.[122]

Sheen at the 81st Academy Awards in 2009. He was invited to join the actors' branch of the Academy in 2007.

2009 saw Sheen portray another public figure; he starred in The Damned United as the outspoken football manager Brian Clough. The Tom Hooper-directed film focused on Clough's disastrous 44-day tenure as manager of Leeds United and marked Sheen's fifth collaboration with writer Peter Morgan. The actor has said Clough is the real-life character he enjoyed playing most.[123] The Guardian, writing in 2009, declared it the "best performance of his big-screen career"[124] while The Times found him "magnificent".[125] USA Today praised "Sheen's masterful performance"[126] while the Los Angeles Times felt he played the role with "a cheeky, dark panache" and "skillful verve".[127] Entertainment Weekly asserted that, despite American audiences' unfamiliarity with Clough, "what's lost in translation is recovered easily enough in Michael Sheen's astonishing performance".[128] Variety noted that his "typically scrupulous channeling of Clough gets the tics and mannerisms right, but also carves a moving portrait of a braggart suddenly out of his depth".[129] Also in 2009, Sheen reprised his role as a werewolf in Underworld: Rise of the Lycans, a prequel to the original film. Of his decision to take part, Sheen has said: "My rule of thumb is that I want to do things I'd like to go and see myself."[130] The New York Times felt he was "the movie’s greatest asset ... [taking] a lively break from his usual high-crust duties to bring wit, actual acting and some unexpected musculature to the goth-horror flick".[131] Variety said he hit "all the right notes in a star-powered performance that will amuse, if not amaze, anyone who only knows the actor as Tony Blair or David Frost"[132] while Richard Corliss of Time noted that he "tries bravely to keep a straight face"[133]

Sheen had a supporting role in 2009's The Twilight Saga: New Moon, the second film in the highly popular vampire series.[134] He was paid a reported £5 million fee and director Chris Weitz has said he "aggressively" pursued the actor for the role.[135] In its review, Rolling Stone said: "Late in the film, a real actor, Michael Sheen, shows up as the mind-reading Aro, of the Italian Volturi vampires, and sparks things up. You can almost hear the young cast thinking, 'Is that acting? It looks hard.' So Sheen is quickly ushered out."[136] While The New York Times said he "preens with plausible menace",[137] USA Today felt he "plays the character with more high-pitched giddiness than menace".[138] He was named Actor of the Year at GQ magazine's annual Men of the Year ceremony.[139] Sheen made two once-off stage appearances in 2009; he performed a scene from Betrayal as part of a Harold Pinter tribute evening at the National Theatre[140] and performed improvisational comedy as part of The Groundlings' Crazy Joe Show in Los Angeles.[141]

Hamlet and Masters of Sex (2010–present)[edit]

In 2010, Sheen had a supporting role in the science fiction sequel Tron: Legacy. Referring to his David Bowie-esque character, Sheen has said, "I was paid to show off basically".[98] The Wall Street Journal found little fun in the movie "except for a gleefully campy turn by Michael Sheen"[142] while The New York Times said he "shows up to deliver the closest thing to a performance in the movie".[143] The Daily Telegraph felt his "lively hamming as a cane-swishing nightclub owner merely underlines how impersonal—how inhuman—much else here is".[144] However, USA Today felt his "scenery-chewing performance ... is meant as comic relief, but this movie thunders along so seriously that the attempt at humor feels jarring".[145] In other 2010 film work, Sheen provided the voices for characters in Tim Burton's Alice in Wonderland[146] and Disney's Tinker Bell and the Great Fairy Rescue[147] and appeared as a terrorist in Unthinkable.[148] On television, Sheen's performance in the third instalment of Peter Morgan's Blair trilogy, The Special Relationship, was nominated for an Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor – Miniseries or Movie.[149] The HBO film examined the "special relationship" between the US and the UK in the political era of Blair and Bill Clinton. It was the sixth collaboration between Sheen and Peter Morgan; both parties have since said they will not work together again "for the foreseeable future".[20][150] Sheen also made a guest appearance in four episodes of NBC's 30 Rock as Wesley Snipes, a love interest for Tina Fey's Liz Lemon. Fey, the sitcom's star and creator, has said that "he was so funny and delightful to work with".[151][152] In November 2010, Sheen received the BAFTA Britannia Award for British Artist of the Year.[153]

In 2011, Sheen starred in and was creative director of National Theatre Wales's The Passion, a 72-hour secular passion play staged in his hometown of Port Talbot, Wales.[154] In addition to a professional cast, over one thousand local amateurs took part in the performance and as many more volunteers from local charity and community groups were involved in preparations in the months leading up to the play.[155][156] The event was the subject of both a BBC documentary and The Gospel of Us, a film by director Dave McKean.[157][158] Sheen has described it as "the most meaningful experience" of his career.[159] The Observer declared it "one of the outstanding theatrical events not only of this year, but of the decade".[160] The Independent's critic described it as "the most extraordinary piece of community-specific theatre I've ever beheld".[161] While the The Daily Telegraph bemoaned the large-scale production's logistical problems, "overall I found it touching, transformative and, in its own wayward way, a triumph."[162] The Guardian felt it was "so much more than just an epic piece of street theatre..transforming and uplifting".[163] Sheen and co-director Bill Mitchell were jointly honoured as Best Director at the Theatre Awards UK 2011.[164] In 2013, Sheen was nominated for a Best Actor Welsh BAFTA for The Gospel of Us.[165]

Sheen's most notable film appearance of 2011 was a supporting role in Woody Allen's Midnight in Paris.[166] Allen noted that "Michael had to do the pseudo-intellectual, the genuine intellectual, the pedant, and he came in and nailed it from the start".[167] Sheen enjoyed playing "someone who’s just absolutely got no sense that he’s overstepping the mark or that he’s being a bore."[168] The film opened the 2011 Cannes Film Festival and became Allen's highest grossing film to date.[169] Also in 2011, Sheen starred in Beautiful Boy, an independent drama focusing on the aftermath of a school shooting,[170] voiced the enigmatic and mysterious villain House in the Doctor Who episode The Doctor's Wife written by his friend Neil Gaiman[171] and made cameo appearances in The Twilight Saga: Breaking Dawn Part 1[172] and Resistance.[173] In 2012 film work, Sheen starred opposite Toni Collette in the independent comedy Jesus Henry Christ[174] and reprised his role as the vampire Aro in the final instalment of The Twilight Saga.[175][176]

Sheen played the title role in Hamlet at the Young Vic in late 2011 and early 2012,[177] a role he first explored in a 1999 BBC Radio 3 production.[178] While there had been tentative plans over the years for both Peter Hall and Michael Grandage to direct Sheen in the play,[179][180][181] he eventually asked Ian Rickson.[140] Rickson's production was set in the secure wing of a psychiatric hospital and featured original music by PJ Harvey.[182] The Evening Standard declared Sheen's performance "an audacious achievement" that "will live in the memory"[183] while The Independent praised "a recklessly brilliant and bravura performance."[184] The Telegraph felt that Sheen "could be right up there among the great Hamlets"[185] while The Times found him "unbearably moving".[186][187] The Guardian described him as "fascinating to watch ... intelligent, inventive and full of insights ... [he] delivers the "What a piece of work is man" passage with a beautiful consciousness of human potential."[188] The Observer declared him an actor "always worth crossing a principality to see and hear" whose ""To be or not to be" is a marvel."[189]

In September 2013, Sheen began starring in Showtime's twelve-episode drama Masters of Sex.[190] He and Lizzy Caplan portray the 1960s human sexuality pioneers Masters and Johnson; the series chronicles "their unusual lives, romance and pop culture trajectory, which saw them go from a Midwestern teaching hospital to the cover of Time magazine and Johnny Carson’s couch".[191] The pilot episode was directed by John Madden.[192] He received a Golden Globe nomination for his performance.[193]

Also in 2013, Sheen appeared in the comedy Admission as Tina Fey's "stiff British boyfriend".[194] Sheen will next appear in the fantasy children's film Mariah Mundi and the Midas Box.[195] In early 2014, he will star in IFC's six-episode The Spoils of Babylon, a television parody of classic, sweeping miniseries.[196] Sheen will play the husband of Kristen Wiig's character.[197] In 2014, Sheen will play a supporting role in Kill the Messenger, which tells the true story of reporter Gary Webb's attempts to connect the CIA to the crack cocaine trade only to become the victim of a smear campaign.[198]

Charity work[edit]

Sheen is an ambassador for TREAT Trust Wales, a charity which aims to provide a rehabilitation and therapy centre in the grounds of Swansea’s Morriston Hospital by 2015,[199] and is the Welsh ambassador of FILMCLUB, a charity which offers after-school film clubs to state primary and secondary schools in an effort to improve literacy levels.[200] He is also an ambassador of the environmental charity Keep Wales Tidy.[201] He is a patron of British charities including Scene & Heard,[202] NSPCC's Child's Voice Appeal,[203] Healing The Wounds,[204][205] The Relationships Centre[206] and WGCADA.[207] He has taken part in a number of charity football matches, most notably captaining the winning Soccer Aid 2010 team at Wembley Stadium.[208] He is a patron of the British Independent Film Awards,[209] an ambassador for the Dylan Thomas Prize[210] and vice-president of Port Talbot Town FC.[211]

Personal life[edit]

Sheen does not adhere to any religion, having asked his parents at the age of seven if he could stop attending church.[212] He has criticised the Conservative Party's Big Society initiative, describing it as "a totally cosmetic bit of PR". "Rather than trying to encourage people to do more voluntary work, I would say look at the people who are doing the work already and give them more support."[159]

Relationships[edit]

Sheen had an eight-year relationship with English actress Kate Beckinsale from 1995 until 2003. They met when cast in a touring production of The Seagull in early 1995[33] and began living together shortly afterwards.[213] Beckinsale credited Sheen with saving her from "a hospital for the criminally insane".[214] Their daughter, Lily Mo Sheen, was born in 1999 in London, England.[33] The actress has said she was "embarrassed" that Sheen never proposed[215][216] but felt as if she were married: "If you keep a library book out long enough, you feel it's yours."[217] Their relationship ended January 2003, soon after the couple moved to Los Angeles from London.[33] Beckinsale had persuaded director Len Wiseman to cast Sheen in the film[218] but, while on set, she and Wiseman (who was married) fell in love.[6] She married Wiseman in 2004.[7] Sheen has said he continues to live in Los Angeles to be close to his daughter rather than return to the UK.[219] He remains friends with both Beckinsale and Wiseman.[220] "We were very lucky in that we didn't have an acrimonious split," Beckinsale has said. "We are still very close and [our daughter] sees us around each other."[221] "He's absolutely one of my most favourite people ever."[222] "I love him dearly—I would miss him dreadfully if he wasn't in my life."[223] Sheen has said that Beckinsale "will always be one of the most important people in my life. I have real love for her."[224]

Sheen had a long-distance relationship with English ballet dancer Lorraine Stewart from late 2004 until mid-2010.[225] He was in a relationship with Canadian actress Rachel McAdams from mid-2010 to early 2013.[226][227][228] He began dating American comedian Sarah Silverman in early 2014.[229][230]

Other honours[edit]

Sheen was appointed Officer of the Order of the British Empire in the 2009 New Year Honours list for his services to drama.[231] He was awarded the freedom of the borough of Neath Port Talbot, Wales in 2008 for his services in the field of the dramatic arts.[232][233] He is an Honorary Fellow of the University of Wales, Newport,[234] the Royal Welsh College of Music & Drama[235] Swansea University,[236] Aberystwyth University and Swansea Metropolitan University,[237][238] and has been awarded the James Joyce Award by University College Dublin.[239]

In addition to theatre, film and television credits, Sheen has also appeared in many radio productions, particularly in the early years of his career.
